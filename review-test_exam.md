**C1.** Name the three components of the **CIA triad** and define each in one phrase.
<details>
<summary> Show answer</summary>

**Confidentiality** = no unauthorized disclosure · **Integrity** = no unauthorized modification (status quo) · **Availability** = no unauthorized withholding (access when needed).

<details>
<summary> Show answer</summary>

Code of ethics = high-level **values/principles** that govern **decision-making**; code of conduct = specific **rules** that govern **actions/behaviour** (and usually list discipline).
</details> <br/>

**C3.** Name the **four elements of privacy** and the **two categories** they fall under.
<details>
<summary> Show answer</summary>

*Control of external influences:* **Solitude, Anonymity, Intimacy.** *Control of personal information:* **Reserve.**
</details> <br/>

*Data generation:* Historical, Representation, Measurement. *Model building:* Aggregation, Learning, Evaluation. *Deployment:* Deployment bias.
</details> <br/>

**C5.** Name the **four types of intellectual property**, give the **patent's duration**, and the **three types of patents**.
<details>
<summary> Show answer</summary>

Trade secret, Trademark/Service mark, Patent, Copyright. **Patent ≈ 20 years.** Patent types: **Utility, Design, Plant.**

**C6.** Differentiate **symmetric** from **asymmetric** encryption, and state the main problem with symmetric encryption.
<details>
<summary> Show answer</summary>

**Symmetric** = one shared secret key for encrypt + decrypt (fast). **Asymmetric** = public + private key pair. Symmetric's problem = **key distribution** (passing the key securely).
</details> <br/>

**C7.** Differentiate a **data controller** from a **data processor**; who must notify **SDAIA** of a breach, and within how many hours?
<details>
<summary> Show answer</summary>

**Controller** decides the **purposes & means** (accountable); **processor** acts **on the controller's behalf**. The **controller** must notify SDAIA **within 72 hours** (and notify affected data subjects without undue delay).
</details> <br/>

<details>
<summary> Show answer</summary>

**Egoism** = own interests above all · **Utilitarianism** = the group's greatest good · **Altruism** = good if consequences favour everyone except the actor.
</details> <br/>

## Section D — Case Studies  *(5 × 6 = 30 marks)*
*Identify the issue & stakeholders, apply the named theories/frameworks, then give a clear judgment.*

**D1. (Ethical theories)** A university data team wants to mine students' **public** social-media posts to predict who is at risk of dropping out — **without telling them**. Analyse through **deontology, utilitarianism, and virtue ethics**, then give a judgment.
<details>
<summary> Show model answer</summary>

- *Issue/stakeholders:* consent-free mining of students' public data; students (privacy/autonomy), at-risk students, university, staff.
- *Deontology:* data wasn't given for this purpose → breaches consent/autonomy; **against** unless a genuine duty of care + transparency are honoured.
- *Utilitarianism:* justified **if** it reliably helps at-risk students at low privacy cost; false positives, stigma, and lost trust may outweigh.
- *Virtue ethics:* the intent to help is good, but prudence/respect favour **transparent, proportionate** methods (the mean), not covert surveillance.
- *Judgment:* **conditionally permissible** — only with transparency, data minimization, notice/consent where feasible, and PDPL compliance; covert mining fails.
</details> <br/>

**D2. (Privacy / SDAIA / GDPR)** A Saudi fintech app suffers a breach exposing customers' national IDs and account balances. Management wants to patch it quietly. As the **DPO**, state what must happen under **SDAIA**, and analyse via **deontology and consequentialism**.
<details>
<summary> Show model answer</summary>

- *SDAIA duty:* the **Controller** must notify **SDAIA within 72 hours** of a breach likely to harm data subjects, **notify affected customers** without undue delay, then **contain and document** (the three stages). A quiet fix is **non-compliant**.
- *Deontology:* honesty + customers' **right to know** → disclose.
- *Consequentialism:* concealment risks fraud, penalties, and lost trust that outweigh short-term reputation → disclose + mitigate.
- *Judgment:* **notify SDAIA and customers, contain, remediate.** Concealment is impermissible.
</details> <br/>

**D3. (ML bias)** A bank's loan-approval model trained on **20 years of past approvals** rejects far more applicants from a minority area. Identify the likely **source(s) of harm**, then judge whether to deploy it using **utilitarianism** and **justice/deontology**.
<details>
<summary> Show model answer</summary>

- *Harm sources:* **Historical bias** (20 yrs of biased approvals); possibly representation/measurement; **deployment bias** if used as-is.
- *Utilitarianism:* aggregate "gains" mask severe harm to the minority group → likely **net-negative** once equity/trust are counted.
- *Justice/deontology:* systematically disadvantaging a group violates the **duty of justice/fairness** regardless of totals → **impermissible**.
- *Judgment:* **don't deploy as-is** — rebalance data, validate on a representative population, run a fairness audit, keep human oversight.
</details> <br/>

**D4. (Intellectual property)** An employee leaves Company A and at Company B (i) reuses A's **secret recommendation algorithm**, (ii) copies A's **curated customer database**, and (iii) **reverse-engineers** A's *publicly released* app. Which actions are IP violations? Analyse via **deontology and virtue ethics**.
<details>
<summary> Show model answer</summary>

- *(i) Secret recommendation algorithm:* a **trade secret** → reusing it breaches confidentiality/NDA → **violation**.
- *(ii) Curated customer database:* a curated/organised database **can be protected** → copying likely **infringes** (plus privacy/PDPL issues).
- *(iii) Reverse-engineering a public app:* generally **allowed** against a trade secret **unless** a patent/licence/contract bars it → **not a violation by itself**.
- *Deontology:* honesty + promise-keeping (NDA) → (i) and (ii) breach duty; (iii) (absent contract/patent) does not.
- *Virtue ethics:* integrity/fairness → exploiting a former employer's secrets is dishonest/unjust.
- *Judgment:* **stop using A's trade secret and database**; reverse-engineering is defensible only if unencumbered.
</details> <br/>

**D5. (Cybersecurity ethics)** A hospital introduces a very secure but **slow login**; doctors say it delays emergency care. Explain the ethical tension (**security vs other values**) and recommend an approach using the course's **best practices**.
<details>
<summary> Show model answer</summary>

- *Issue/stakeholders:* **security vs usability/other values** (patient care); patients, doctors, hospital, security team.
- *Tension:* cybersecurity is costly and consumes resources; **usability concerns can't justify weakening** security, **but** finding the right **balance** (resource allocation) is itself an ethical issue — weigh **harms, benefits, rights, and values**.
- *Best practices:* consider the **human lives** behind the system, **design for security AND usability**, **invite stakeholder (clinician) input**, and plan for **crisis/emergency** access.
- *Judgment:* don't weaken security outright; adopt a **usable-secure** solution (e.g., MFA + audited emergency "break-glass" access), involving clinicians and weighing harms/benefits before deciding.
</details> <br/>
