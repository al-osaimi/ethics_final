## Quiz 1

**Q1.** Which is an example of a **data availability** violation?
- A) Phishing
- B) Unauthorized access
- C) Data tampering
- D) DDoS attack on a website
- **Answer:** **D** — a DDoS floods a service so legitimate users can't reach it (availability). Phishing/unauthorized access → confidentiality; tampering → integrity.
- **Reference:** Past Quiz 1 Q1 · Week 4 (CIA triad).

**Q2.** Primary focus of a **code of ethics**?
- A) Operational procedures
- B) Specific rules
- C) Guiding principles and values
- D) Legal compliance
- **Answer:** **C** — ethics = values that guide decisions; the others describe a *code of conduct* or compliance.
- **Reference:** Past Quiz 1 Q2 · Week 2 (Codes of Ethics).

**Q3.** A student copies from an online journal without citation (KKU code of ethics). This is:
- A) Acceptable if public
- B) Depends on performance
- C) Minor if it helps learning
- D) A violation of academic-integrity policies
- **Answer:** **D** — plagiarism breaches integrity regardless of source or intent.
- **Reference:** Past Quiz 1 Q3 · Week 2 (codes/academic integrity).

**Q4.** Per **consequentialism**, collecting user data without consent is ethical if:
- A) Legal only
- B) Follows company policy
- C) Respects rights
- D) It increases overall benefits more than harms
- **Answer:** **D** — consequentialism judges by **outcomes**; A–C are non-consequentialist criteria.
- **Reference:** Past Quiz 1 Q4 · Week 2 (Consequentialism).

**Q5.** Which best describes **ethics**?
- A) Personal belief system
- B) Legal framework
- C) Moral principles guiding behavior
- D) Rules for financial success
- **Answer:** **C**.
- **Reference:** Past Quiz 1 Q5 · Week 2.

**Q6.** A company hides a minor breach. From a **deontological** standpoint it should:
- A) Inform customers because honesty is a moral duty
- B) Act on financial consequences
- C) Stay silent if damage is minimal
- D) Wait for media
- **Answer:** **A** — deontology = act from duty, independent of consequences.
- **Reference:** Past Quiz 1 Q6 · Week 2 (Deontology).

**Q7.** *True/False:* "Authentication is a process that ensures **data integrity** in the CIA triad."
- **Answer:** **FALSE** — authentication verifies **identity/authenticity**; **integrity** (data not modified) is a separate CIA pillar, ensured by hashing. *(The CIA distinction is the point being tested.)*
- **Reference:** Past Quiz 1 Q7 · Week 4 (CIA / authentication / hashing).

**Q8.** Which document is more likely to include **disciplinary actions** for violations?
- A) Vision statement
- B) Mission statement
- C) Code of ethics
- D) Code of conduct
- **Answer:** **D** — conduct = specific enforceable rules + penalties.
- **Reference:** Past Quiz 1 Q8 · Week 2 (ethics vs conduct).

**Q9.** Role of **privacy policies**?
- A) Encourage breaches
- B) Inform users about data usage
- C) Promote sharing without consent
- D) Eliminate consent
- **Answer:** **B**.
- **Reference:** Past Quiz 1 Q9 · Week 8 (privacy/disclosure).

**Q10.** Example of **institutional** privacy being violated?
- A) A company's confidential budget report is hacked
- B) A staff member's health condition is disclosed
- **Answer:** **A** — institutional = the *organization's* data; option B is *individual* privacy.
- **Reference:** Past Quiz 1 Q10 · Week 8 (institutional vs individual privacy).

## Quiz 2

**Q1.** *True/False:* once an AI-algorithm **trade secret is leaked publicly** (employee leaves / Samsung–ChatGPT), it loses protection.
- **Answer:** **TRUE** — trade-secret protection depends on secrecy; once public, it is gone.
- **Reference:** Past Quiz 2 Q1 · Week 9 (Trade Secret).

**Q2.** Theory treating **character** as the primary subject of ethics:
- A) Virtue Ethics
- B) Consequentialism
- C) Human Nature
- D) Hedonism
- **Answer:** **A** — Virtue Ethics centres on character/*arete*.
- **Reference:** Past Quiz 2 Q2 · Week 6 (Virtue Ethics).

**Q3.** Strategy to **detect bias after deployment**:
- A) Freeze the model
- B) Ignore feedback
- C) Continuous monitoring and auditing
- D) Shrink the dataset
- **Answer:** **C** — post-deployment drift/bias is caught by ongoing monitoring/audits.
- **Reference:** Past Quiz 2 Q3 · Week 5 (deployment bias / best practices).

**Q4.** ❗ How does a **Data Officer** balance privacy (personal vs business needs)?
- A) Collect all data
- B) Avoid documentation
- C) Remove all access
- D) Enforce policies that protect personal data while allowing necessary business uses
- **Answer:** **D** — the DPO **balances**, not blocks; A–C defeat the role.
- **Reference:** Past Quiz 2 Q4 · Week 8 (data controller/officer).

**Q5.** *True/False:* **Trademarks** can protect the **underlying AI algorithm** itself.
- **Answer:** **FALSE** — trademarks protect brand identifiers; the algorithm is protected (if at all) by **patent** or **trade secret**.
- **Reference:** Past Quiz 2 Q5 · Week 9 (IP types).

**Q6.** ❗ A hiring dataset is mostly successful candidates from **one demographic**. Likely outcome:
- A) Ignores demographics
- B) Deletes data

**Q7.** Most secure password against brute force:
- A) CIS6231
- B) Passwords password

- **Reference:** Past Quiz 2 Q7 · Week 16 (security best practices) + Hive Systems 2025 chart.

**Q8.** On the **Virtue Continuum**, the virtuous action is:
- A) None of the above
- B) Following laws strictly
- C) Ignoring moral values
- D) Balance between two extremes
- **Answer:** **D** — virtue = the **mean** between deficiency and excess (e.g., courage between cowardice and foolhardiness).
- **Reference:** Past Quiz 2 Q8 · Week 6 (Virtue Continuum / Aristotelian mean).

**Q9.** In a sensitive clinical survey, use **anonymity** to:
- A) Protect participants' identities
- B) Track progress
- C) Increase ads
- D) Share publicly
- **Answer:** **A**.
- **Reference:** Past Quiz 2 Q9 · Week 8 (anonymity).

**Q10.** Per the **SDAIA Disclosure Cases Guideline**, disclosing only "the minimum personal data necessary to fulfil the purpose" is the principle of:
- **Answer:** **Data minimization** — collect/process/disclose only the minimum needed for the defined purpose.
- **Reference:** Past Quiz 2 Q10 · SDAIA *Personal Data Disclosure Cases Guideline*.

# Section C — Short Questions (predicted)
*SDAIA (S8, S9) and GDPR (S10) are here per the exam format, plus S11 ties them together.*

**S1. Distinguish a code of ethics from a code of conduct.** `[Code of ethics vs conduct | High]`
- **Answer:** A **code of ethics** is a high-level statement of **values/principles** that **governs decision-making** (the "why"; e.g., the ACM Code). A **code of conduct** is a set of **specific, enforceable rules** that **govern actions/behaviour** (the "what/how"; the document more likely to list **disciplinary actions**).
- **Reference:** Week 2 (Codes of Ethics) + recap. 

**S2. Name and define the three components of the CIA triad.** `[Security | High]`
- **Answer:** **Confidentiality** = no unauthorised *disclosure*; **Integrity** = no unauthorised *modification* (status quo maintained); **Availability** = no unauthorised *withholding* (access when needed).
- **Reference:** Week 4 (Security/CIA). 

**S3. List the four IP types and the criteria for comparing them; give the patent duration and the key trade-secret feature.** `[IP | High]`
- **Answer:** Four: **trade secret, trademark/service mark, patent, copyright**. Compared by **public domain, duration, application**. **Patent** ≈ **20 years** (utility/design/plant). **Trade secret** **never expires while secret**, reverse-engineering allowed, lost once public.
- **Reference:** Week 9 (IP). 

**S4. Name the Seven Sources of Harm in ML, grouped by lifecycle stage.** `[Bias | High]`
- **Answer:** *Data generation:* Historical, Representation, Measurement. *Model building:* Aggregation, Learning, Evaluation. *Deployment:* Deployment bias.
- **Reference:** Week 5 (Framework for Sources of Harm). 

**S5. State the four elements of privacy and the two categories they fall under.** `[Privacy | High]`
- **Answer:** *Control of external influences:* **Solitude** (be alone), **Anonymity** (no public identity), **Intimacy** (not monitored). *Control of personal information:* **Reserve** (control of one's personal info and its dissemination).
- **Reference:** Week 4 (Privacy). 

**S6. Define anonymity and distinguish pseudo-identity from untraceable identity.** `[Privacy | Medium]`
- **Answer:** **Anonymity** = absence of identity / being nameless. **Pseudo-identity** = identified by a pseudonym/code (pen-name); the most common, potentially linkable variant. **Untraceable identity** = known by no name at all, not linkable.
- **Reference:** Week 2 (Anonymity). 

**S7. Differentiate a data controller from a data processor; who must notify SDAIA of a breach, and when?** `[Privacy/SDAIA | High]`
- **Answer:** **Controller** = decides the **purposes and means** of processing and is **accountable**; **processor** = processes data **on the controller's behalf**. The **controller** must notify **SDAIA within 72 hours** of awareness of a breach likely to harm data subjects, and notify affected data subjects without undue delay.
- **Reference:** Week 8 (data controller) + SDAIA Breach Guide; controller/processor framing .

**S8. Summarize the SDAIA Personal Data Disclosure Cases: when may a controller disclose personal data, and what limits apply?** `[SDAIA | High]`
- **Answer:** A controller may disclose in **six cases**: (1) **consent** of the data subject; (2) data from a **publicly available source**; (3) request by a **public entity** for **public interest/security/implementing another law/judicial requirements**; (4) to **safeguard public health, public safety, or the life/health of specific individuals**; (5) **de-identified subsequent processing** (does not identify the subject); (6) the controller's **legitimate interests** (not for *sensitive* data). **Limits across all cases:** a **defined purpose**, **due diligence** to protect privacy, and **data minimization** (minimum data necessary). The guideline is **non-binding**; the **PDPL** is the reference.
- **Reference:** SDAIA *Personal Data Disclosure Cases Guideline*. 

**S9. Under the SDAIA breach guide, who notifies whom, in what time, and what are the three response stages?** `[SDAIA | High]`
- **Answer:** The **Controller** notifies **SDAIA within 72 hours** of becoming aware of a breach likely to harm data subjects (via the National Data Governance Platform) and notifies affected **data subjects** without undue delay. Three stages: **(1) SDAIA Notice → (2) Breach Incident Containment → (3) Documentation.** Built on the **PDPL** (Royal Decree M/19, amended M/148); key roles: Controller, Data Subject, **DPO**.
- **Reference:** SDAIA *Personal Data Breach Incidents — Procedural Guide* (Oct 2024). 

**S10. What is the GDPR, and what are its controller/processor roles and breach-notification timelines?** `[GDPR | High]`  **[beyond course materials]**
- **Answer:** The **GDPR** is the EU's comprehensive data-protection regulation (in force 2018) protecting individuals' personal data, with extraterritorial reach. **Controller** = decides the purposes/means of processing; **processor** = processes on the controller's behalf. **Breach notification:** the controller notifies the **supervisory authority within 72 hours** (Art. 33) and notifies **affected individuals without undue delay where the risk is high** (Art. 34). Core principles (Art. 5) include lawfulness/fairness/transparency, **purpose limitation**, **data minimization**, accuracy, storage limitation, integrity & confidentiality, and **accountability**.
- **Reference:** GDPR (EU) Arts. 5, 33, 34. 

**S11. Give two similarities and two differences between SDAIA/PDPL and the GDPR.** `[SDAIA + GDPR | High]`  **[beyond course materials]**
- **Answer:** **Similarities:** both are **comprehensive** data-protection regimes using **controller/processor** roles and a **72-hour** breach-notice rule; both require **data minimization** and protect data-subject rights. **Differences:** the **PDPL** allows up to **30 days (extendable)** to answer data-subject requests vs the GDPR's up to **~3 months** for complex requests; and the PDPL **bars the legitimate-interest basis for *sensitive* data**, with KSA-specific governance (SDAIA registration, Saudi cross-border-transfer rules).
- **Reference:** PDPL/GDPR comparative analyses.

# Section D — Case Studies (predicted)
*Structure: **Issue & stakeholders → each theory → judgment.** Covers the blueprint's required privacy/SDAIA/GDPR, ML-bias, and IP scenarios, plus a flagship ethical-theories case.*

**CS1 — Data-driven outreach (flagship ethical-theories case).** `[Ethical theories | High]`
- **Scenario:** A children's-services team mines parents' **public** social-media posts to find families likely to need help — *without contacting them first*. Helpful reach, or intrusive surveillance? Analyse via deontology, utilitarianism, and virtue ethics.
- **Issue & stakeholders:** Is consent-free mining of public data for outreach justified? Stakeholders: at-risk children/families, parents (privacy/autonomy), the agency/staff, funders/public, regulators.
- **Deontology:** using data not knowingly given for this purpose risks breaching **consent/autonomy** → leans **against** unless a real protective duty + transparency are honoured.
- **Utilitarianism:** justified **if** it reliably reaches vulnerable children at low privacy cost; false positives and lost trust could flip the calculus.
- **Virtue ethics:** the **intention to help** is virtuous, but **prudence/temperance** counsel proportionate, transparent methods (the mean) over covert surveillance.
- **Judgment:** **Conditionally permissible** — only with a genuine duty, demonstrable net benefit, transparency/minimization/consent where feasible, and PDPL compliance. Covert, consent-free mining fails deontology and prudence.
- **Reference:** Week 6 (Virtue Ethics — data-targeting example) + Week 2. 

**CS2 — Health-tech breach (privacy / SDAIA / GDPR).** `[Privacy/SDAIA/GDPR | High]`
- **Scenario:** A breach exposes patients' names, IDs, lab results. Engineers want to quietly patch; the DPO says notify. Analyse via deontology and consequentialism alongside the SDAIA/GDPR duty.
- **Issue & stakeholders:** Must the breach be disclosed? Stakeholders: patients (data subjects), company, DPO, SDAIA, public.
- **Regulatory frame:** Controller must notify **SDAIA ≤72h** and **data subjects without undue delay** (GDPR Art. 33/34 parallel). 
- **Deontology:** honesty + respect for autonomy → patients have a **right to know** → disclose.
- **Consequentialism:** concealment's downstream harms (fraud, penalties, lost trust) outweigh short-term reputation → disclose + mitigate.
- **Judgment:** **Notify SDAIA and patients promptly; contain and remediate.** Concealment is impermissible.
- **Reference:** SDAIA Breach Guide ; GDPR Arts. 33/34 ; Week 4/8.

**CS3 — Biased ICU-triage model (ML bias).** `[Bias/Harm | High]`
- **Scenario:** A bed-prioritisation model trained on historical data and validated on a skewed benchmark under-prioritises a minority group. Identify the harm sources; analyse via utilitarianism and deontology.
- **Issue & stakeholders:** Is deploying/continuing it ethical? Stakeholders: patients (esp. the disadvantaged group), clinicians, hospital, society.
- **Harm sources:** **Historical** (legacy data) + **Evaluation** (skewed benchmark) + **Deployment** (used out of fit).
- **Utilitarianism:** aggregate "gain" masks severe distributive harm → likely net-negative once equity/trust are counted.
- **Deontology/justice:** disadvantaging a group by protected characteristic violates the duty of **justice**, regardless of totals → impermissible.
- **Judgment:** **Suspend/correct** — rebalance, representative validation, fairness audit, datasheets/model cards, human oversight.
- **Reference:** Week 5 (Seven Sources of Harm). 

**CS4 — Departing employee & proprietary pipeline (IP).** `[IP | High]`
- **Scenario:** A scientist leaves Company A and at Company B reuses A's **confidential pipeline** and a **curated dataset**, and **reverse-engineers** A's *public* model. Analyse the IP issues and via deontology and virtue ethics.
- **Issue & stakeholders:** Which uses infringe? Stakeholders: Company A, Company B, the scientist, the field.
- **IP frame:** pipeline = **trade secret** (breach of confidentiality/NDA); a curated **database** can be protected (copying likely infringes); **reverse-engineering a public release is generally allowed** *unless* a patent/licence/contract bars it.
- **Deontology:** duties of honesty + promise-keeping (NDA) → reusing the secret/database breaches them; reverse-engineering a public model (absent contract/patent) does not.
- **Virtue ethics:** integrity/fairness → exploiting a former employer's secrets is dishonest/unjust.
- **Judgment:** Stop using A's **trade secret** and **database**; the reverse-engineering is defensible only if unencumbered by patent/licence/contract.
- **Reference:** Week 9 (IP).
